Mariana Matrix: Uncovering the Mystery Behind the Iconic Superhero!

Mariana Trench, also known as the Mariana Trench, is the deepest part of the ocean on Earth. It is located in the western Pacific Ocean and stretches approximately 2,550 kilometers (1,580 miles) long and has an average width of 69 kilometers (43 miles).

The deepest point of the trench is known as the Challenger Deep and has a depth of 10,994 meters (36,070 feet). The Mariana Trench was first identified by Commodore Charles Wilkes during his United States Exploring Expedition in 1838. Since then, several expeditions have been conducted to explore it. The first successful mission to reach the bottom of the trench was led by Swiss oceanographer Jacques Piccard and Lt. Don Walsh on January 23rd 1960. With their bathyscaphe Trieste they made it to a depth of 10,916 meters (35,814 feet). The trench is home to a diverse range of animal species including giant amphipods, crabs, shrimps, flatfish and even some species of sharks such as the six gill shark and frilled shark. The conditions in the Mariana Trench are extreme; it is pitch dark and temperatures range between 1-4°C. There is also high pressure due to depths that can reach up to 1 tonne per square inch! The Mariana Trench has been declared an international marine protected area by an agreement between Japan and United States in 2009. This agreement has been instrumental in protecting this unique part of our planet from human interference such as deep sea mining or fishing activity. The Mariana Trench offers us an insight into our aquatic habitats that are still relatively unexplored; its extreme depths hold mysteries that scientists are only beginning to unveil. Its protection ensures that these mysteries will remain intact for future generations to explore and uncover!
